Fall 2014

Session 1: Bubbles

How do bubbles form?Why are bubbles usually round? Can you blow a bubble inside of a bubble? Could you make a square bubble? These and many other questions will be answered as students learn about isoperimetric inequalities, minimal surfaces, higher dimensions, and polyhedra by building plastic models and blowing bubbles.

Session 2: Crystals

Geometric actually occur in nature. Many mineral crystals grow into regular, geometric shapes. Students will learn about patterns, symmetries, and polyhedra by playing with plastic models and looking at a number of real crystal specimens.

Session 3: Keplerâ€™s Cosmos

German mathematician and astronomer Johannes Kepler believed that the locations of the planets were related mathematically. He thought that the order of the solar system was based on properties of the five Platonic solids. By trying and failing to prove this, he paved the way for modern astronomy. Students learn about polyhedra, symmetry, and Euler's characteristic through playing with several different models of the Platonic solids.

Session 4: Snowflakes

What is snow? Students will learn about patterns, polyhedra, and symmetries in 2 and 3 dimensions by playing with plastic models and looking at magnified photos of snowflakes. This session nicely wraps up the GEMS Workshop as we will learn about bubbles in ice, ice crystals, and a paper by Kepler on the symmetries of snowflakes.
